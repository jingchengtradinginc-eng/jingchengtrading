How to Choose Takeout Packaging for Busy Kitchens

Match containers, lids, and bags to your food type, service style, and delivery distance.

← Back to guides
Packaging 5 min read

The wrong container costs you more than just money — it costs you a customer. Leaked soup, crushed fries, a lid that won't stay on: these are the things that turn a first-time order into a last one. Getting packaging right is one of the simplest things a restaurant or deli can do to protect its reputation.

Start with the food, not the container

Every packaging decision should start with what's going inside, not with what's cheapest or most available. Ask yourself three things: Is it hot or cold? Is it liquid or solid? How far is it traveling?

Hot, liquid foods (soups, stews, braised dishes)

These need containers that seal tightly and hold heat without softening. UNICUP deli soup containers in 12oz, 24oz, and 32oz are purpose-built for this — they're rigid PP plastic with a secure snap lid. For lighter volumes or deli counters, 8oz, 16oz, and 32oz SPT/SQT soup cups work well for fast service.

Hot, solid foods (rice dishes, noodles, fried items)

The FH black rectangular containers (the FH-B series in 150-set cases) are a go-to for meal prep and restaurant takeout. Black reads as premium, the lids snap cleanly, and the containers stack efficiently. For portion sizes, match the container volume to your typical serving — overfilling causes lid failure.

Cold foods (salads, cut fruit, sushi, sandwiches)

Hinged clear containers let the customer see what they're buying before they open it, which matters at deli counters and grab-and-go setups. Clear hinged deli containers in 12oz, 24oz, and 32oz work for most cold applications. For eco-conscious customers, the 6×9" sugarcane HC96 hinged clam is compostable and looks great for upscale takeout.

Rule of thumb: If the food has sauce, liquid, or steam — go rigid with a snap lid. If it's dry and structural (like a sandwich or pastry) — a hinged clamshell or paper box works fine.

Match lids carefully

A container without a matching lid is a liability. Before you order, confirm your lids match your containers by size and brand where possible. Mixing container brands often means lids that fit loosely or not at all.

  • Plastic lids for rectangular aluminum pans (705, 747, 788 series) sold separately — make sure you order both
  • Round aluminum pan lids come in foil board or plastic — foil board for catering, plastic for tighter sealing
  • PET dome lids (10–24oz) fit a range of cup sizes, useful if you use multiple cup sizes

Think about the bag last — but don't skip it

Customers carry containers in bags. A leaking container in a flimsy bag still ruins the experience. Match bag strength to order weight.

  • Light individual orders → paper bags #6 or #8
  • Medium orders with handles → SS paper handle bags (small 8×4×10, medium 10×5×13)
  • Large multi-item orders → large handle bag 13×7×17 or the 10×8×24 heavy duty bag
  • Grocery or retail → 1/6 or 1/8 happy face bags hold a lot and are very cost-effective per case

Delivery distance changes everything

A container that's fine for a two-block walk may fail on a 30-minute delivery. For delivery orders traveling more than 15–20 minutes, prioritize containers with secure lids, avoid overfilling, and use heavier bags. Double-bagging soup orders is always worth it.

Quick tip: If you do both in-house dining and delivery, keep two container sizes for the same dish — smaller for dine-in, larger with a more secure lid for delivery. The extra cost is minimal compared to a refund.
