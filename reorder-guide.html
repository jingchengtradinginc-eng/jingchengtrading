A Simple Reorder Checklist for Restaurant Disposables

Build a repeatable weekly routine so you never run short on containers, bags, gloves, foil, or paper.

← Back to guides
Operations 6 min read

Most supply shortages in a restaurant aren't caused by bad luck — they're caused by no system. When you're ordering reactively (running to the storage room Wednesday morning and realizing you have three bags of containers left), you're always one busy weekend away from a problem. A consistent weekly check takes less than ten minutes and eliminates most of it.

When to check — and when to order

Pick one day per week as your inventory check day. Many operations do it Monday morning when the previous week's numbers are fresh. Walk through storage, count open cases, and compare against your typical weekly usage. The rule of thumb: if you have less than two weeks of supply on hand, it's time to order.

Delivery threshold: Our minimum for delivery is $400 in product or 15+ cases. Plan your orders to hit this — consolidating two smaller orders into one weekly order saves you pickup trips and often saves money on per-case cost.

The weekly checklist by category

Go through each category below. For each one, note how many open cases you have and estimate how many you'll need before your next order arrives.

Containers

Check these first — highest turnover
  • Main takeout containers
    Your most-used FH black or white series — count open cases vs. weekly usage
  • Soup containers
    UNICUP 12oz, 24oz, 32oz — especially important for delis and lunch counters
  • Lids
    Always check separately — lids often run out faster than containers
  • Hinged containers
    If you do grab-and-go or deli display, these go fast

Bags

Easy to forget until you're out
  • Handle bags (S, M, L)
    High turnover — most operations go through a case of medium bags every few days
  • Shopping bags (1/6 or 1/8 size)
    Supermarkets and delis use hundreds per day
  • Paper bags #6 and #8
    Bakery, deli counter, and dry goods

Gloves & PPE

Critical for food safety compliance
  • Nitrile gloves (all sizes in use)
    Blue or black — check S, M, L, XL separately. Running out of one size stops that person from working

Foil & Wrap

Heavy and bulky — order ahead
  • Foil rolls (12" and/or 18")
    These are heavy per case — factor in delivery weight
  • Pre-cut foil sheets
    If you use them for wrapping, they go fast at a deli counter
  • Aluminum pans and lids
    Rectangular (705, 747, 788) and round (7", 8", 9") — check each size you use
  • Wax paper / no-wrap / film
    Often overlooked until mid-service

Napkins & Paper

Low cost, high volume
  • C-fold and dinner napkins
    Cases are large — store in a dry area
  • Multi-fold paper towels
    Check all bathroom and kitchen dispensers
  • Tissue and bath tissue
    Easy to let slip below one case

A few things that trip up most operations

Ordering containers without matching lids

This is the most common mistake. Containers and lids are often sold separately. If you order 150 rectangular containers but forget the lids, you have 150 containers you can't use for takeout. Always add both to your order at the same time.

Not accounting for seasonal spikes

Summer means more takeout, more catering, more events. Holidays mean bulk orders from restaurants that cater family meals. Look back at what you used the same week last year, and order 15–20% more going into a known busy period.

Storing in a humid or hot area

Paper products absorb moisture. Napkins, paper bags, and tissue stored near a dishwasher or in an un-air-conditioned back room will degrade faster. Keep them in a cool, dry spot — ideally off the floor on shelving.

Bottom line: The restaurants and delis that never run out of supplies aren't better at ordering — they're just more consistent about checking. Build the habit, stick to the same day each week, and it becomes automatic within a month.
